## Abstract

How does a liberal democracy sustain petroleum extraction at historically high levels while simultaneously performing global leadership in climate transition? This paper argues that Norway's case is not a paradox to be resolved but a structure to be explained. Drawing on two complementary bodies of evidence — a systematic corpus analysis of 15,210 Norwegian parliamentary speeches (2016–2025) using the POLARIS Corpus (POLARIS, v1.0) and twenty semi-structured interviews with civil society actors, politicians, civil servants, and researchers — we demonstrate that disavowal (*Verleugnung*: *I know very well, but nevertheless…*), in Zupančič's Lacanian formulation, operates in Norway not as individual rhetorical failure but as a structural political achievement distributed across institutional sites, discursive positions, and subjective investments.

The corpus provides five interlocking empirical findings: (1) the probability of a parliamentary speech naming petroleum declines by 11.4% per year (OR = 0.886, p < 0.001) while extraction continues at record levels; (2) party membership explains approximately zero percent of variance in petroleum mention rates, confirming that the pattern is structural rather than ideological; (3) the Norway Government Pension Fund Global (NBIM) — built entirely on petroleum revenues — uses the term "carbon neutral" at 51.7 times Parliament's rate while naming "petroleum," "oil," and "extraction" at zero frequency; (4) parliament persistently names the fund as *Oljefondet* (Oil Fund) rather than by its official de-oiled title — preserving petroleum as historical achievement while suppressing it as ongoing activity; and (5) the "both/and" co-occurrence of petroleum and climate language in the same speech rises sharply, suggesting that disavowal has become an increasingly managed, explicit discursive performance.

The qualitative evidence maps four interlocking mechanisms that reproduce this structure: a myth archive of half-truths that closes every objection gap; institutional arrangements that generate disavowal as an automatic organizational outcome; geopolitical re-legitimization that absorbs new crises without changing the extractive trajectory; and affective capture that makes petroleum identity feel like national selfhood rather than policy choice. Zupančič's Ethical Square maps how twenty informants across four structural positions — state actors (forced choice), insider NGOs (excluded choice), critical outsiders (excluded choice, lack in Other), and industry-adjacent pragmatists (forced choice, lack in subject) — each contribute a specific form of knowing-while-not-acting that reproduces the paradox without any actor choosing it.

The paper concludes that Norway's green-extractive formation is not hypocrisy but constitutive contradiction: "green" and "extractive" are not opposing categories that happen to coexist but mutually sustaining conditions that require each other. What the corpus measures is the discursive surface of a structure that the interviews illuminate from within. Together, they map the most thoroughly documented case of climate disavowal available in the literature.

**Keywords**: disavowal, extractivism, Norway, parliamentary discourse, Ethical Square, Zupančič, corpus linguistics, STM, green identity, petrostate

## 1. Introduction: The Norwegian Paradox as Structural Achievement

Norway presents itself globally as a climate leader and model of ecological responsibility. It finances the world's largest sovereign wealth fund. It has committed to net-zero by 2030. It hosts major international environmental conferences, funds tropical forest conservation programmes across three continents, and consistently ranks among the top performers in global sustainability indices. At the same time, it is the world's seventh-largest oil exporter and third-largest gas exporter. Its petroleum industry has never had more active production licences than it does today. The same government that set the 2030 net-zero target approved new Arctic drilling permits within twelve months of doing so.

This is what journalists call the Norwegian paradox. Scholars in discourse analysis and environmental politics have documented it extensively, tracking how the paradox is managed through narrative strategies, institutional design, and cultural mythology (Ihlen, 2006; Norgaard, 2011; Anker, 2020; Sæther, 2017). What they have shown is *that* the paradox persists and *some* of the strategies that manage it. What remains underdeveloped is a structural account of *why* it is so resistant to disruption — why factual correction does not dissolve it, why government changes do not shift it, why activist pressure does not dislodge it, why even the most sophisticated critics of Norway's extractivism find themselves reproducing its conditions.

This paper argues that the Norwegian case is not a paradox awaiting resolution but a structural achievement. Drawing on Alenka Zupančič's (2024) formulation of disavowal — *je sais bien, mais quand même* (I know it very well, but still…) — we argue that the Norwegian state has not resolved the contradiction between green identity and petroleum extraction. It has organized the contradiction symbolically so that it is livable without being resolved. This organization is not cynical performance but genuine inhabitation: actors across all positions simultaneously hold the contradiction and cannot act on its full weight because their structural positions foreclose the action that knowledge would demand.

This argument requires two kinds of evidence. The first is *corpus* evidence: systematic, quantitative demonstration that disavowal operates across the whole of Norwegian parliamentary discourse — not only in particular speeches, certain parties, or particular sessions, but as the structural tendency of the institution as a whole. The second is *ethnographic* evidence: accounts from within the system of how actors inhabit contradictory positions, what makes them livable, and how they are rationalized. The POLARIS Corpus (POLARIS, v1.0) provides the first; twenty semi-structured interviews with civil society actors, politicians, civil servants, and researchers conducted between May 2025 and November 2025 provide the second.

The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 introduces the theoretical framework. Section 3 describes data and methods. Sections 4–8 present the five key corpus findings. Section 9 maps the four structural mechanisms of disavowal from the qualitative evidence. Section 10 applies the Ethical Square. Section 11 synthesizes and concludes.

## 2. Theoretical Framework: Disavowal and the Ethical Square

The concept of disavowal (*Verleugnung*), developed by Freud in the context of fetishism, was appropriated by Lacan and extended by Žižek into a general account of ideological functioning. Disavowal names a structure in which a subject acknowledges a truth and simultaneously acts as if that truth carries no consequence: *I know very well that the fund was built on oil extraction, but nevertheless I behave as if it is a responsible long-term investment vehicle with no particular relation to the petroleum industry*.

Žižek's formulation — "they know very well what they are doing, but they do it anyway" — captures the structure but treats it as uniform: a single psychological state, distributed across individuals. What Zupančič's (2024) development adds is structural differentiation. Disavowal, she argues, is not primarily a psychological mechanism but a political one: it is produced by the field of structural positions that subjects occupy, rather than by individual choices. The knowing dimension and the *quand même* dimension are distributed across different positions and different actors in a system organized to accommodate them without any single position bearing the full weight of the contradiction.

Zupančič's Ethical Square (2012) provides the map of how this distribution works. Two intersecting axes produce four structural positions. The first axis distinguishes between *lack in the subject* — actors experience the problem as residing in their own inadequate capacity, resources, or courage — and *lack in the Other* — actors attribute the problem to systems, institutions, or structural conditions that prevent the right action. The second axis distinguishes between *forced choice* — actors must choose even though every available option involves some ethical compromise — and *excluded choice* — the most desirable option is institutionally unavailable, not merely difficult.

The four positions this generates are:
- **Forced choice + lack in Other** (state and political actors): must choose within a system whose terms they cannot change; attributes failure to structural necessity
- **Excluded choice + lack in subject** (insider NGOs): knows what is needed; institutional constraints foreclose full expression; experiences this as professional obligation
- **Excluded choice + lack in Other** (critical outsiders): the system forecloses adequate demands; circumvents through cultural, legal, or artistic intervention
- **Forced choice + lack in subject** (industry-adjacent pragmatists): no structural alternative currently available; delays adequate demands to technological horizon

The critical theoretical move is that each position reproduces the paradox through a different route, and all four are mutually constitutive: each position forecloses what its neighboring positions need, while simultaneously providing what the system as a whole requires. No single actor produces the paradox. The paradox is an emergent property of the field.

This framework guides both the corpus analysis — which tests whether the discursive patterns confirm structural uniformity across party positions — and the qualitative analysis, which maps where each informant sits and what that position makes livable.

## 3. Data and Methods

### 3.1 The POLARIS Corpus (POLARIS, v1.0)

POLARIS comprises plenary debate transcripts from the Stortinget (Norwegian Parliament) for all nine parliamentary sessions from 2016–17 through 2024–25 (N = 15,210 keyword-matched speeches from ~103,000 total extracted speeches), collected via the Stortinget public API. The corpus begins in 2016 — the year of Norwegian ratification of the Paris Agreement — the moment Norway formally committed to a green identity while structurally accelerating petroleum extraction. The keyword filter requires the presence of at least one term from the theoretical terrain of the Norwegian paradox: *petroleum*, *olje* (oil), *klima* (climate), *klimaendring* (climate change), *fornybar energi* (renewable energy), *bærekraft* (sustainability), *energiomstilling* (energy transition), *Oljefondet/NBIM*. Extended keywords (*karbonfangst*, *grønn vekst*, *rettferdig omstilling*) trigger inclusion but are flagged separately. This filter is not topical but theoretical: inclusion means a speech operates in the ideological terrain where petroleum and green identity collide.

A Phase 2 secondary corpus comprises 19 NBIM (Norges Bank Investment Management / Government Pension Fund Global) annual and responsible investment reports (2015–2024), collected from nbim.no. Cross-institutional comparison between Parliament and NBIM constitutes a central analytic.

The corpus is publicly available at: https://github.com/odripads/polaris

### 3.2 Computational Methods

Analysis proceeds through three layers:

**Descriptive**: frequency analysis by session and party; KWIC (keyword-in-context, ±8 tokens; N = 1,997 petroleum instances, 22,854 klima instances); collocational profiling (±5 tokens); Structural Topic Modelling (STM, K=10, Spectral initialization, prevalence covariates: year_scaled, party indicators; Roberts et al. 2019).

**Inferential**: logistic regression predicting petroleum mention from year, party, and speech type (N = 14,868 speeches; robust SEs, HC3); Mann-Kendall non-parametric trend test (Kendall's τ on annual petroleum-to-klima ratios); mixed-effects logistic regression with random intercepts for party, session, and speaker (lme4; Bates et al. 2015); STM effect estimation via estimateEffect() with global uncertainty (95% CIs).

**Cross-institutional**: term frequency per 10,000 words compared between Parliament (Phase 1) and NBIM (Phase 2) for a theoretically motivated vocabulary.

Inferential statistics are applied under the superpopulation model standard in corpus linguistics (Gries 2021): speeches are treated as draws from the distribution of all possible Norwegian parliamentary speech, with effect sizes and confidence intervals as primary outputs.

### 3.3 Qualitative Methods

Twenty semi-structured interviews were conducted between May and November 2025 with a theoretically purposive sample spanning five actor categories: civil society organizations and NGOs (Framtiden i våre hender, ForUM, Klimakultur, Legenes klimaaksjon, WWF Norway, Changemaker, Greenpeace Norway, Norges Fiskarlag), industry-adjacent civil society (LO Norway, Naturvernforbundet Trondheim), state-owned enterprise (Avinor), government (Norway's Ministry of Climate and Environment, Ministry of Local Government), political parties (Green Party Trøndelag, Labour Party Standing Committee on Energy and Environment, Conservative Party Standing Committee on Energy and Environment, senior Liberal Party), and expert/academic (Oil Change International, Norwegian Climate Foundation, University of Bergen). All informants are anonymized; names are plant-based pseudonyms. Interviews were between 45 and 110 minutes and were conducted in English or Norwegian with transcription. Analysis proceeded through Zupančič's Ethical Square as an explicit coding framework applied post-hoc to the full interview corpus.

## 4. The Declining Petroleum Signifier

The most direct corpus evidence of disavowal is the trajectory of petroleum-to-climate speech ratios across nine parliamentary sessions (2016–2025). Within the POLARIS filtered corpus — speeches already selected because they contain at least one keyword from the paradox terrain — petroleum language declines year on year while climate language remains stable or grows.

**Table 1: Petroleum and klima speech rates by session (% of corpus speeches)**

| Session | Petroleum speeches | Klima speeches | Petrol:Klima ratio |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2016–17 | 154 (8.7%) | 916 (51.8%) | 0.168 |
| 2017–18 | 135 (8.9%) | 796 (52.7%) | 0.170 |
| 2018–19 | 150 (8.0%) | 1,157 (61.9%) | 0.130 |
| 2019–20 | 166 (9.8%) | 1,030 (60.5%) | 0.161 |
| 2020–21 | 98 (5.5%) | 1,106 (62.0%) | 0.089 |
| 2021–22 | 103 (6.0%) | 1,113 (64.9%) | 0.093 |
| 2022–23 | 72 (4.2%) | 911 (53.6%) | 0.079 |
| 2023–24 | 73 (4.8%) | 865 (56.6%) | 0.084 |
| 2024–25 | 69 (4.2%) | 831 (50.7%) | 0.083 |

Even within speeches that already discuss energy, petroleum, or climate, petroleum is spoken about 3-to-12 times less often than climate. The ratio halved across the corpus period: 0.168 in 2016–17 to 0.083 in 2024–25. Over the same period, Norwegian petroleum extraction continued at historically high levels — record production in several years.

The Mann-Kendall test confirms the decline is monotonic: τ = −0.722, p = 0.009 (two-tailed). Nine sessions; no reversal. The logistic regression estimates this effect at an odds ratio of **0.886 per year** [95% CI: 0.863–0.909, p < 0.001]: each additional year reduces the odds of a given speech mentioning petroleum by 11.4%, controlling for party and speech type.

This is the disavowal signal formalized: the thing Norway extracts at the highest rate in its history is the thing its parliament speaks of less, year on year, controlling for all other factors. The activity is present in the world; its name is progressively absent from discourse.

The compounding is itself analytically significant. In Norwegian parliamentary speech, *petroleum* almost never appears as a standalone word. It appears embedded in bureaucratic compounds: *petroleumsvirksomhet* (petroleum operations), *petroleumsnæringen* (the petroleum industry), *petroleumstilsynet* (the Petroleum Safety Authority), *petroleumspolitikk* (petroleum policy), *petroleumsskatten* (petroleum taxation). Norwegian compounding domesticates — it makes a concept part of an established institutional landscape rather than a thing in itself. Petroleum is not *utvinnet* (extracted) in parliamentary speech; it is *forvaltet* (managed), *regulert* (regulated), *skattlagt* (taxed). The collocational profile of petroleum — "norsk," "næringen," "viktig," "sektoren," "aktivitet" — is the lexicon of governance, not extraction. Contrast this with the collocational profile of *klima*: "regjeringen," "klimapolitikk," "klimamålene," "klimagassutslipp" — the language of aspiration, commitment, and moral urgency. The semantic structure of disavowal: the activity is *administered*, the identity is *performed*.

## 5. Cross-Partisan Consistency: Disavowal is Not a Party Position

The structural claim of disavowal — that it is a property of the state apparatus, not of particular parties — is directly testable against the corpus. If disavowal is structural, petroleum mention rates should be broadly consistent across parties regardless of ideological position.

**Table 2: Vocabulary rates by party (% of speeches mentioning each term)**

| Party | Position | N speeches | Petroleum rate | Klima rate |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| FrP | Right-populist, pro-petroleum | 1,145 | 9.5% | 49.0% |
| H | Centre-right | 1,858 | 6.8% | 50.8% |
| KrF | Centre-right Christian | 423 | 7.3% | 61.0% |
| A | Centre-left (governing) | 2,099 | 5.5% | 56.8% |
| Sp | Agrarian centre | 1,252 | 4.9% | 57.7% |
| SV | Left | 1,736 | 6.2% | 70.3% |
| MDG | Green | 763 | 5.5% | 75.0% |
| R | Far-left | 481 | 5.2% | 46.4% |
| V | Green liberal | 1,101 | 6.4% | 70.9% |
| Statsråd (Ministers) | Government capacity | 4,010 | 7.7% | 50.3% |

The Progress Party (FrP) — which explicitly defines itself as pro-petroleum, frames extraction as national pride, and routinely opposes climate restrictions — speaks about *klima* in nearly half of its speeches (49.0%) and about petroleum in only 9.5%. The ratio within FrP's own corpus is roughly 5:1 in favour of climate language. The party most ideologically committed to petroleum has been structurally compelled to perform the same green identity as its opponents.

The two Prime Ministers across the corpus period — Erna Solberg (Conservative, 2013–2021) and Jonas Gahr Støre (Labour, 2021–present) — are functionally indistinguishable in their petroleum/klima profiles: Solberg 2.6%/56%, Støre 1.6%/57%. These are political opponents from different ideological traditions governing through different coalitions. The institutional position of Prime Minister produces the same discursive performance regardless of who holds it.

The mixed-effects logistic regression makes this precise. With random intercepts for party, session, and speaker, the **party-level intraclass correlation is approximately zero** — party explains ~0% of the variance in petroleum mention rates once individual speaker variation is controlled. The session-level ICC is 0.4%. The speaker-level ICC is 26.7%, indicating that some individuals are rhetorically distinctive (certain FrP backbenchers, certain SV climate specialists), but these are individual habits distributed *within* parties, not party-level patterns.

The year fixed effect remains large and significant in the multilevel model: OR = 0.89 [0.846–0.936], p < 0.001. The decline is temporal, not partisan. It is the shared trajectory of an institution, not the strategy of a faction.

The STM party contrast confirms the same picture differently. FrP shows a significantly higher prevalence of T5 (Adversarial floor debate, +0.093, p < 0.001) and T1 (Petroleum fiscal regime, +0.041, p < 0.001) compared to the rest of the corpus. But even this FrP distinctiveness operates within the field: FrP occupies the adversarial position *within* the debate, challenging from the petroleum-affirmative side, but the debate itself is already defined by the green-extractive frame. Disavowal is not FrP's position or Labour's position — it is the debate.

## 6. The "Both/And" Frame: Holding the Contradiction Explicitly

The 415 speeches (2.7% of the corpus) that name both *petroleum* and *klima* in the same text are the densest sites of disavowal — the moments where a speaker explicitly holds both terms and must manage their co-existence. A further 125 speeches (0.8%) achieve what we call the triple co-occurrence: petroleum, klima, *and* fornybar (renewable) in a single text.

The rate at which petroleum mentions also contain klima — conditional on petroleum being mentioned — rises from 30% in 2016–17 to over 55% by 2021–22. By mid-corpus, when petroleum is mentioned in parliament, it is accompanied by climate language in more than half of cases. The thing cannot be named without immediately managing it with the green supplement.

Four discursive frames manage the co-occurrence:

**The sequentialist frame**: petroleum is Norway's present; renewables are Norway's future. The transition is underway; extraction continues while the alternative is prepared. *"Vi skal klare å kombinere petroleumsvirksomheten med en aktiv klimapolitikk"* (We must manage to combine petroleum operations with an active climate policy). The "both/and" is a temporal promise: we will do both, and eventually only the latter.

**The expertise-transfer frame**: skills, infrastructure, and industrial capacity from petroleum can be redirected to offshore wind and carbon capture. The petroleum past becomes the foundation of the green future. Nothing is lost; everything continues. Nettle (Greenpeace) and Weld (Norwegian Climate Foundation) document how this framing structures investment decisions: "there's always a new justification, always someone else worse off."

**The global-responsibility frame**: Norway's petroleum revenue finances the green transition — domestically through welfare-state investment, internationally through the Oil Fund's ESG criteria. The extraction is legitimated by what it funds. *"Det er det som gir oss muligheten til å satse på fornybar energi"* (It is this [petroleum revenue] that gives us the opportunity to invest in renewable energy).

**The adversarial-accountability frame**: used primarily by SV, MDG, and occasional A backbenchers, petroleum is named in order to *expose* the disavowal — *"Dere kan ikke kalle dere et klimaparti og samtidig åpne nye oljefelter"* (You cannot call yourselves a climate party while simultaneously opening new oil fields). This naming is the critical outsider position — but even this naming is contained within the institution, the corpus, the same discursive field. The exposure is registered; the structure is not disrupted.

## 7. Cross-Institutional Disavowal: NBIM as the Completion of Parliament's Operation

The most dramatic quantitative evidence comes from the cross-institutional comparison. The Norway Government Pension Fund Global (NBIM) is the institutional site where petroleum extraction revenues are narratively transformed: from fossil-fuel income into long-term, responsible, globally diversified capital stewardship. This transformation requires a specific discursive operation — the suppression of origin.

**Table 3: Key term rates per 10,000 words — Parliament (2022–24) vs. NBIM Responsible Investment Reports (2022–24)**

| Term | Parliament | NBIM | Ratio |
|---|---|---|---|
| carbon neutral / net zero | 0.29 | 14.9 | **51.7×** |
| responsible | 1.76 | 65.6 | **37.3×** |
| divestment | ~0.0 | 30.5 | **~3,000×** |
| sustainable / bærekraft | 9.52 | 28.6 | 3.0× |
| climate / klima | 38.0 | 66.1 | 1.74× |
| petroleum | 3.3 | **0.0** | Parliament names it more |
| oil / olje | 7.56 | **0.0** | Parliament names it more |
| extraction | 0.85 | **0.0** | Parliament names it more |

NBIM uses "carbon neutral / net zero" at 51.7 times Parliament's rate. "Responsible" appears 37.3 times more frequently. "Divestment" — the fund's ethical-exclusion mechanism — appears at approximately zero frequency in Parliament (which mandated it) but at 30.5 per 10,000 words in NBIM's own reports. Parliament mandated the mechanism; NBIM enacts it; Parliament does not speak of it.

The symmetrical finding: *petroleum*, *oil*, and *extraction* all rate zero in NBIM's self-description. The fund that was built by and exists entirely because of petroleum revenue does not name petroleum, oil, or extraction in its institutional discourse. Parliament — the chamber that oversees petroleum licensing, production, and taxation — names these terms more than the fund they produced.

This constitutes the cross-institutional form of disavowal. Parliament cannot fully suppress the naming of petroleum because it must legislate it. NBIM can suppress it entirely because its mandate is stewardship, not production. The petroleum money arrives at NBIM already laundered of its origin. NBIM's task is not to name it but to invest it responsibly. The suppression of "extraction" in NBIM discourse is not dissembling — it is the institutional completion of the disavowal that Parliament begins. Two institutions, two phases of the same operation: Parliament manages the production and cannot not-name it; NBIM manages the proceeds and must not name it.

## 8. The Name of the Fund: Temporalised Disavowal

The fund has two names. *Oljefondet* — the Oil Fund — is the popular form: historically accurate, etymologically honest. *Statens pensjonsfond utland* (SPU — Government Pension Fund Global) is the official form, adopted in 2006: bureaucratically neutral, origin-suppressing, globally legible.

Over the corpus period, *Oljefondet* appears as 689 tokens versus 410 for SPU/Statens pensjonsfond — a ratio of 1.68:1. Parliament persistently uses the name that names the oil. The share oscillates but shows no monotonic decline: in 2023 and 2024, Oljefondet's share of fund references actually increases to 78% and 77%.

This complicates the simple disavowal narrative in a theoretically productive way. Parliament does not suppress the fund's oil origin at the level of the proper noun. It keeps naming the thing an Oil Fund. What it suppresses is the *activity* — the ongoing extraction, the wells, the production, the barrel counts, the platform operations. The fund's origin is preserved as historical fact — a monument, a proud achievement, something Norway *owns* and that bears its name — while the present tense of petroleum extraction is progressively disappeared from discourse.

The disavowal is thus temporally structured: *petroleum* as ongoing activity is suppressed; *Oljefondet* as inheritance from that activity is retained. Norway knows it is a petrostate — *it was*, and the fund is the evidence. But the *is* — the current drilling, the licensing rounds, the platform expansions — that is what must not be spoken. The Oil Fund becomes the alibi of a historical past rather than the current proceeds of a present activity. Disavowal here works through *temporalisation*: petroleum is something Norway *had*, that generated a fund it *has*, not something it *is still doing*. This temporalisation is what Weld (Norwegian Climate Foundation) identifies in the interviews as a consistent pattern: "the myths contain a grain of truth, but are blown out of proportion."

## 9. The Four Structural Mechanisms of Disavowal

The corpus documents the surface — the discursive pattern of a structure that the qualitative evidence illuminates from within. Twenty interviews across five actor categories identify four interlocking mechanisms that reproduce this structure without requiring any individual to choose it.

### 9.1 The Myth Archive and Its Half-Truths

Norwegian petroleum justification is organized around five interlocking narratives that form what we call a myth archive — not a collection of outright lies but of half-truths amplified beyond their analytical warrant, each closing a different objection gap.

**Myth 1: Clean oil** — Norwegian extraction has genuinely lower production-phase emissions than the global average (~2–3% of lifecycle emissions). The myth generalizes this fact to the other 97%: "our oil is cleaner" becomes a license to continue supplying. Daisy (Framtiden i våre hender): "the production is slightly cleaner, it's true, but when the oil is burned, the result is the same." Spurge (Conservative MP): "Norwegian production has very low emissions compared to other offshore producers." The half-truth is real; its amplification to a blanket justification is the myth.

**Myth 2: Oil saves the welfare state** — SSB found as early as 2010 that Norway "barely lives off oil revenues." The fund's capital is invested internationally; domestic welfare is funded by labour productivity and taxation. Yet surveys by WWF-Norway show 80–90% of Norwegians believe welfare is funded directly from oil revenues. Poppy (WWF Norway): "when asked how wealthy Norway was before the discovery of oil, the overwhelming majority estimated their country to be 70th or 80th in the world, when Norway was actually the 10th richest globally per capita." The myth works through affect rather than fact; correction does not dissolve it.

**Myth 3: Supply-side neutrality** — if Norway does not supply the oil that European markets demand, someone with lower environmental standards will. Broom (Labour MP, Standing Committee): "Norway will continue producing oil and gas as long as there is demand for it, and if Norway stops while demand remains, someone else will sell it." Rushes (Ministry of Climate and Environment): "within the framework of the Paris Agreement commitments, the private sector bears responsibility for extraction decisions." The argument depoliticizes the production decision, transforming a political choice into an obligation to market forces.

**Myth 4: Demand-side responsibility** — production is virtuous because Norway simultaneously works to reduce global fossil fuel demand. The same Labour MP simultaneously advocates continued Norwegian production while working internationally for demand reduction. The argument claims credit for demand-side activism while absolving the supply side of equivalent obligations.

**Myth 5: The new climate denial** (Thistle, Klimakultur) — unlike classical denial, this form accepts climate science but insists Norway cannot phase out now because of the energy security crisis. "People are no longer saying that climate change isn't real; rather, they insist that Norway simply cannot phase out oil now because of its crucial role in the global energy supply." Classical denial is becoming empirically untenable; the energy security argument refreshes the justificatory infrastructure.

What unites these five myths is their closed-system structure: each covers a different objection. The clean oil myth closes criticism of the extraction process. The welfare myth closes economic alternatives. Supply-side neutrality closes production responsibility. Demand-side responsibility closes diplomatic accountability. The new climate denial closes temporal urgency. Factual correction cannot dismantle this system because the myths do not invite evaluation of individual claims — they organize existing facts into a structure where each claim lends credibility to the others.

### 9.2 Institutional Arrangements That Produce Disavowal Automatically

Four institutional arrangements generate disavowal as a structural organizational outcome, independently of individual intentions.

**The ministerial split**: the Ministry of Climate and Environment and the Ministry of Petroleum and Energy hold irreconcilable mandates. Clover (ForUM): "the division within the government structure is confusing because the ministries are talking with two tongues, saying different things in different directions." Rushes: "responsibility for the oil sector rests with the Ministry of Energy, and when it comes to decisions about drilling policy, it falls to them." The arrangement means the state can simultaneously announce ambitious climate targets (Ministry of Climate) and approve new exploration permits (Ministry of Energy) without these actions registering as contradictory in any single institutional space. The system distributes contradictions structurally across ministries in a way that prevents them from crystallizing into political crisis.

**Passive ownership of Equinor**: the Norwegian state holds 67% of Equinor but practices what Vetch (Oil Change International) describes as "ownership without responsibility." At Equinor's 2024 AGM, a proposal reaffirming parliament's own ownership policy received 20% support from non-state shareholders — and the Norwegian government, as majority owner, voted against it. The state used its ownership position to defeat its own stated policies. This is not a contradiction but an institutional convention: the passive ownership model, designed to prevent political interference in commercial decisions, simultaneously prevents the state from exercising the ownership rights it would need to align Equinor with Paris Agreement commitments.

**Scope 3 accounting exclusion**: Norway's NDC covers only domestic territorial emissions. The 97–98% of lifecycle emissions from Norwegian oil and gas that are released when those exports are burned in European furnaces, power stations, and vehicles do not appear in Norwegian climate accounting. This technical accounting convention renders the most consequential part of Norway's climate impact structurally invisible to domestic policy. Rushes: "95 to 98% of emissions from Norway's oil and gas industry are generated abroad, when these exports are burned." The accounting framework makes a comprehensive policy response institutionally unavailable.

**Co-optation ecology**: Equinor funds approximately 125 million NOK annually in university partnerships (NTNU agreement, renewed 2024), employs ~300 communications staff dedicated to reputation management, funds newspaper podcast segments without editorial oversight (Yarrow, Legenes klimaaksjon), and sponsors cultural and sporting events across Norwegian civil society. The 78% petroleum tax deductibility means the public subsidizes this communications infrastructure at a rate nearly four times more favorable than renewable energy companies. Nettle (Greenpeace): "due to the way the Norwegian tax system works, the oil industry can write off 80% of all costs related to sponsorship and advertising, while renewable energy companies can only write off 22%." Meanwhile, major NGOs (Rainforest Foundation Norway received 125 of its 151 million NOK from Norad in 2014) are financially dependent on the state they are supposed to oversee. Clover (ForUM): "ForUM is largely government-funded. How can we take a clear stance?" The co-optation ecology narrows what can be said publicly about Norwegian oil without career or financial consequences.

### 9.3 Geopolitical Re-legitimization as Periodic Refresh

Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 provided the most powerful re-legitimization of Norwegian extraction in the modern period. Almost overnight, the primary discourse shifted from climate leadership to energy security. What previously required complex discursive work — proving that petroleum extraction was compatible with climate commitment — suddenly became a security obligation. Weld (Norwegian Climate Foundation): "the oil and gas sector straightened its back and said that finally they could be proud again."

Norway's pipeline gas volume to Europe reached 111 billion cubic meters by 2024, filling a significant portion of the gap left by Russian gas collapse (from 155 billion to under 20 billion cubic meters). Norwegian officials reframed themselves as "a reliable, trustworthy, and long-term energy partner for Europe" — deploying the language of interpersonal virtue to describe commercial supply relationships. Broom (Labour MP): "since 2021 and the outbreak of the war against Ukraine, many European colleagues have argued that Norway must continue producing gas because it is their lifeline, and politicians from all camps, including the greens, understand the importance of a stable and reliable energy source."

The geopolitical re-legitimization does not create a new justificatory structure. It absorbs a new crisis into the existing apparatus. The same narratives that previously absorbed clean oil arguments, welfare arguments, and supply-neutrality arguments now absorb energy security arguments with equal ease. The content of the justification changes; the extractive trajectory does not shift in the slightest.

Vetch (Oil Change International) provides the sharpest formulation: "Norwegian politicians are hoping, and essentially betting, that the EU will fail" on its 2040 climate targets, because countries have repeatedly failed to meet climate ambitions in the past. Rushes effectively confirms this from within state circles: "the issue of stranded assets is not being addressed enough, and Norwegian policy seems to be betting that the 2040 target will fail and everything will work out in the end." The energy security narrative provides the moral cover; the Green Paradox (Sinn, 2009) provides the economic logic: when resource owners anticipate future regulation, they advance extraction to monetize assets before the regulatory window closes.

### 9.4 Affective Capture and the Foreclosure of Post-Oil Imagination

The three preceding mechanisms — myth archive, institutional arrangements, geopolitical re-legitimization — are primarily discursive and institutional. They operate through what actors say and what structures inhibit. A complete account must also address the affective dimension.

The petroleum industry has become woven into Norwegian self-understanding at a depth where its criticism feels like an attack on national identity rather than a policy disagreement. Prof. Tansy (University of Bergen, ClimLife): "elderly participants reacted to Greta Thunberg's criticism with anger because it threatened not a policy position but rather an identity narrative — the story of how Norway transformed into what it is today." The repeated invocation of Brundtland's phrase "it is typically Norwegian to be good" — a pre-emptive defense of extractivism as global virtue — exemplifies how exceptionalism serves as affective scaffolding.

Moss (Naturvernforbundet Trondheim, municipal planner): "after North Korea, Norway is the most conformist population in terms of willingness to deviate from social consensus." Janteloven's imperative — "don't believe you know more than we do" — de-authorizes expert voices challenging oil centrality by framing climate advocacy as arrogant individualism. Rem (Green Party activist) describes breaking social norms around oil as more stressful than the legal consequences of civil disobedience.

The deepest affective anchor is what Valerian (LO Norway) articulates from the labor movement: "entire local communities on the west coast would cease to exist if oil and gas disappeared today." The west coast communities organized around the oil services industry face not just employment loss but the dissolution of their social fabric — schools, municipal services, collective identity. Just transition, in this framing, is not primarily a policy design challenge but an imaginative one. The absence of credible public answers to these questions serves as an affective barrier to phase-out politics: the questions are not merely economic but existential.

## 10. The Ethical Square: Mapping the Closed System

The Ethical Square maps how twenty informants across four structural positions contribute their specific form of knowing-while-not-acting to the reproduction of the paradox.

**State and political actors** (Rushes, Broom, Spurge, Mallow) occupy forced choice combined with lack in the Other. They must choose within a system whose terms they do not define; they attribute the failure to achieve adequate climate action to geopolitical demands, parliamentary constraints, and supply-demand dynamics that make unilateral phase-out seem futile. Rushes, after fourteen years as a senior civil servant in the Ministry of Climate and Environment, acknowledges that stranded assets are not sufficiently discussed, that Norwegian policy appears to be betting on EU climate target failure, and that "it is very difficult to change things in politics if you don't feel compelled to do so." This is not cynicism — it is a structural description of forced choice from within a system that generates no compelling mechanism.

Broom's formulation — "Norway will continue producing oil and gas as long as there is demand for it, and if Norway does not, someone else will" — is not a rationalization when understood from the forced choice position. It is a structural description of what is available to act upon. The supply-side neutrality myth from section 9.1 is simultaneously a myth from the outside and a genuine constraint description from within.

**Insider NGO actors** (Daisy, Clover, Poppy, Sorrel) occupy excluded choice combined with lack in the subject. They know what an adequate climate response requires but their institutional position — financial dependence on state funding, need to maintain credibility with stakeholders operating within the oil consensus — makes that adequate response structurally unavailable. Daisy knows precisely what arguments the climate situation demands; she consistently deploys financial and economic arguments because those are the ones her funders and stakeholders will accept. This is not a compromise but, within the structural position, a form of professional competence. Sorrel (Changemaker) refrains from civil disobedience not from lack of personal conviction but because it would jeopardize her organization's standing with state funders. Clover (ForUM): "ForUM is largely government-funded. How can we take a clear stance?"

**Critical outsiders** (Thistle, Heather, Nettle, Rem) occupy excluded choice combined with lack in the Other. The correct response is excluded not by personal limitations but by structural conditions: the political and institutional architecture forecloses adequate demands through normal channels. Thistle's cultural interventions through Klimakultur, Heather's periods of civil disobedience, Nettle's litigation strategy through Greenpeace are all workarounds specific to a position defined by its exclusion from channels that might otherwise legitimize adequate demands. The system knows full well that Thistle's diagnosis of "new climate denial" is empirically sound; it excludes it from official debate by placing it in a position whose institutional reach does not threaten consensus.

**Industry-adjacent pragmatists** (Sedge, Valerian, Bracken) occupy forced choice combined with lack in the subject. No structural alternative currently exists at the scale demanded by their sector; their own position within or adjacent to extraction-dependent industries leads them to experience transition as something that must happen but cannot be demanded from where they sit. Valerian (LO): "a just transition is not primarily an environmental question for me — it is a question of who gets the contract for the installation of offshore wind turbines; because the alternative, supporting a phase-out without an answer about what happens to its members, is a forced choice not permitted by the labor movement's structural obligations."

The analytically decisive finding is what this mapping reveals collectively. Each position reproduces the paradox through a different route, and all four are mutually constitutive: the state actor's forced choice forecloses supply-side demands, making the insider NGO's financial arguments necessary as a second-best substitute. The insider NGO's excluded choice forecloses civil disobedience, making the critical outsider's workaround approaches necessary. The critical outsider's institutional marginalization absorbs challenges without structural change, enabling the pragmatist's deferral to a technological horizon. And the pragmatist's just-transition framework provides state actors with the content they need to maintain the appearance of movement without any movement taking place.

The technological panacea — CCS, green hydrogen, offshore wind expertise transfer — is the phantasmatic object where all four positions converge. State actors need it to postpone structural questions. Insider NGOs need it to maintain the credibility of incremental advocacy. Critical outsiders need it to be credible enough to be worth challenging. Pragmatists need it to defer adequate demands to horizons beyond their professional reach. The knowledge elite (researchers at oil-funded universities) produce the panacea's credibility within an epistemic regime organized by oil capital — not through bad faith but through forced choice within a funding environment that cannot produce conclusions demanding the system's termination.

The Oil Fund is the material anchor of the closed system. Every Norwegian professional across all four quadrant positions is a beneficiary of a fund that converts extraction into welfare. This material stake constitutes the deepest structural reason why no single quadrant position generates a phase-out demand: professional integrity across all four positions is bought, at the material level, by the same capital that finances the extraction activities each actor might privately question.

## 11. Discussion: Corpus and Interviews in Dialogue

The corpus findings and interview evidence are not parallel analyses — they illuminate a single formation from two directions.

The corpus measures the discursive surface: the declining petroleum vocabulary, the cross-partisan uniformity, the rising both/and co-occurrence, the institutional asymmetry between Parliament and NBIM, the temporalised fund naming. These are aggregate patterns across hundreds of speakers, nine sessions, and fifteen thousand speeches. They confirm that disavowal is *structural* in the technical sense: it is not reducible to individual rhetorical choices or party strategies but characterizes the institution as a whole.

The interviews explain the mechanism: they show *why* the structure is so resistant to disruption. Every actor across all four quadrant positions holds climate knowledge with sincerity. None of them suppresses what they know — all twenty informants demonstrated clear understanding of the contradiction. What the Ethical Square reveals is that each position couples knowledge with a specific form of inability to act on its full weight. This coupling is experienced not as constraint but as professional obligation, civic virtue, or institutional competence. Disavowal is not chosen; it is the structural achievement of an organized field.

The fund naming finding bridges the two levels with particular elegance. The corpus shows parliament persistently using *Oljefondet* rather than SPU — preserving petroleum as historical achievement while the discourse of ongoing extraction declines. The interview evidence explains the affective mechanism: the Oil Fund is how every Norwegian professional, regardless of position, maintains material investment in the continuation of extraction. Naming it as the Oil Fund preserves the pride of origin while depoliticizing the present. Temporalisation — petroleum as what Norway *had*, not what it *is still doing* — is how the macrostructure of disavowal organizes itself across discourse and institutional practice simultaneously.

The paper's central claim — that the Norwegian green-extractive formation is not contradiction awaiting resolution but constitutive co-production — is confirmed by both bodies of evidence. The corpus demonstrates that "green" and "extractive" language co-evolve rather than one displacing the other: climate language grows as petroleum language declines, and the STM topic structure shows energy transition absorbing rather than replacing petroleum. The interviews demonstrate that "green" means four different things depending on quadrant position — extraction efficiency (state actors), financial risk (insider NGOs), supply reduction (critical outsiders), and managed transition (pragmatists) — and that the emptiness of the signifier is the condition for surface consensus. What holds the Norwegian green-extractive formation together is not agreement but the shared name, empty enough to accommodate irreconcilable investments.

What Zupančič's framework demonstrates that discourse analysis alone cannot is that this emptiness is not merely rhetorical but structural: it is produced by the field of positions, maintained by the complementarity of four forms of knowing-while-not-acting, and sustained materially by the Oil Fund as the conversion mechanism that makes extraction feel like welfare. The phase-out constitutes what Lacan called the Real — the thing that the current symbolic order cannot symbolize without ceasing to be itself.

## 12. Conclusion

This paper has presented the first systematic combination of corpus-computational and ethnographic evidence for the structural character of disavowal in Norwegian petroleum discourse. The key findings:

1. **Lexical suppression**: Petroleum mention rates decline by 11.4% per year (OR = 0.886, p < 0.001) while extraction continues. The activity is present in the world; its name is progressively absent from parliamentary discourse.

2. **Structural uniformity**: Party membership explains approximately zero percent of variance in petroleum mention rates. The discursive pattern is an institutional achievement, not a partisan position. Even the explicitly pro-petroleum Progress Party speaks about climate nearly five times as often as petroleum.

3. **Cross-institutional hyper-performance**: NBIM, the fund built on petroleum revenues, uses "carbon neutral" at 51.7 times Parliament's rate while naming petroleum, oil, and extraction at zero frequency. Parliament begins the disavowal; NBIM completes it.

4. **Temporalised disavowal**: Parliament names the fund as *Oljefondet* (Oil Fund), preserving petroleum as historical achievement, while progressively suppressing the ongoing activity. Petroleum is something Norway *had*; the fund is what it *has*. The present tense of extraction is what must not be spoken.

5. **Both/and escalation**: The rate at which petroleum mentions co-occur with climate language rises sharply — by 2021, petroleum is accompanied by climate language in over half of its appearances. Disavowal has become an increasingly explicit, managed performance.

6. **Four-position closed system**: Twenty informants distributed across the Ethical Square's four structural positions each contribute a specific form of knowing-while-not-acting. No single position produces the paradox; the closed system produces it collectively, with each position foreclosing what its neighbors need while providing what the aggregate requires.

The Norwegian case is significant not because it is unique but because it is the most thoroughly documented and the most honest about its own paradox. The same mechanisms — myth archive, institutional layering, geopolitical re-legitimization, affective capture — operate across all major fossil-fuel-producing liberal democracies. Norway is the clearest case precisely because it acknowledges the contradiction most openly, and that acknowledgment is itself part of the system's working mechanism: "a country that sincerely commits to climate targets, genuinely funds renewable research, honestly acknowledges the contradiction, and yet continues expanding extraction has constructed a form of legitimacy more robust than classical denial — it has preemptively incorporated the critique" (Borgen-Eide & Gubrium, 2025).

What this paper ultimately maps is the architecture of a problem that cannot be solved by changing individual attitudes or increasing information — because the mechanism does not reside within individuals. It requires what Zupančič identifies as an act rather than a policy: a rupture in the conditions of the existing order, not a better choice within them. The Norwegian case offers neither a model to replicate nor a failure to correct. It offers the clearest map available of how a liberal democracy constructs, sustains, and rationalizes a green identity that makes extractivism socially legitimate — and maps what would structurally be required to make that map actionable.
